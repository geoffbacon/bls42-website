Speakers

Meghan Sumner, Stanford University

Meghan Sumner is Associate Professor in the Department of Linguistics at Stanford University. Her primary research interest centers on how listeners move from sound to meaning when faced with a highly variable speech signal. Recently, she has focused on integrating social information conveyed through speech into current theories of perception, recognition, and encoding of spoken sounds and words.

Judith Tonhauser, The Ohio State University

Judith Tonhauser is Associate Professor in the Department of Linguistics at The Ohio State University. She conducts research in cross-linguistic semantics and pragmatics, with an empirical focus on Paraguayan Guaraní, a Tupí-Guaraní language spoken in Paraguay. Recent research topics include presuppositions and other projective contents, focus and prosody, and temporal and nominal reference.

Joe Pater, University of Massachusetts, Amherst

Joe Pater is Professor in the Department of Linguistics at the University of Massachusetts, Amherst. His current research focuses on the use of weighted constraints for the modeling of phonology and its learning.

Joey Sabbagh, University of Texas, Arlington

Joey Sabbagh is Associate Professor in the Department of Linguistics at the University of Texas, Arlington. He received his Ph.D. in Linguistics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 2005 with a dissertation examining the syntax and argument structure of non-verbal predicates in Tagalog. His current research is centered on syntactic theory and the syntax of Austronesian languages, as well as the interfaces of syntax with phonology and semantics.

Liina Pylkkänen, New York University

Liina Pylkkänen is Associate Professor of Linguistics and Psychology and the Director of the NYU Neurolinguistics Laboratory as well as a co-director of the Neuroscience of Language Laboratory at NYU Abu Dhabi. She received her Ph.D. in Linguistics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and conducted her post-doctoral work at New York University. Her research addresses the brain bases of language processing, with a focus on semantic cognition. She is one of the leading researchers in the use of magnetoencephalography (MEG) to study the brain mechanisms of language processing and has published extensively on the neuromagnetism of both word and sentence comprehension, with more recent work extending to language production.
